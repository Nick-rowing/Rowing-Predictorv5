How accurate is the on-water prediction?

It's a heuristic, not a stopwatch. Numbers are calibrated against typical erg-to-water relationships, but real water times depend on technique, crew, rigging, and weather. Treat it as a starting estimate — usually within 5–10 seconds of reality for an experienced rower.

Where do the recruiting benchmarks come from?

The USRowing line uses the official U19 Selection Development Camp standards (M: 6:15–6:30, W: 7:10–7:25). NCAA tiers cross-reference Sparks, NCSA, Crimson Athletics, and Rower Academy's published 2026 figures. See the Sources section at the bottom for direct links.

How does the trajectory projection work?

Year-over-year improvement curves come from typical training-adaptation research: novices drop 15–25 seconds in year one, intermediate athletes 5–10 seconds, advanced athletes 2–4 seconds. Improvement also flattens with age — a 15-year-old still has hormonal growth tailwinds a 21-year-old doesn't.

Why is my 8+ time faster than my erg?

Because eight rowers combine power against a single hull. World-class eights row a 2k around 5:18 — nobody pulls that split on an erg. Smaller boats (singles, pairs) go slower than the erg because you also have to balance, steer, and fight wind.

I'm a lightweight. Does this account for it?

Yes. The weight adjustment penalises rowers above the reference (80 kg M / 65 kg F) because heavier bodies add hull drag. Lightweights also get separate benchmark lines, since D1 lightweight programs apply stricter weight rules (≤72.5 kg M / ≤59 kg W).

What if my erg is great but I'm short, or vice versa?

Coaches care about both. A 6'4" rower at 6:30 has a different ceiling than a 5'10" rower at 6:30 — height correlates with stroke length potential and is hard to develop. But many programs recruit shorter rowers who outwork the field. The training-advice card flags whichever factor is hurting your prediction most.
